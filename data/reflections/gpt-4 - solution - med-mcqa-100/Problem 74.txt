The patient's symptoms of recurrent aspirations suggest a problem with swallowing. The Superior Laryngeal Nerve (SLN) innervates the cricopharyngeus muscle, which is a part of the upper esophageal sphincter. Dysfunction of this muscle due to SLN injury can lead to aspiration. The patient's voice has not changed, which suggests that the Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN), primarily responsible for voice production, is not injured. Therefore, the most likely injured nerve is the SLN.
